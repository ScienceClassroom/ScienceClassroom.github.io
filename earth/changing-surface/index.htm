Q&A


What is the role of weathering in recycling Earth materials?

Breaking down rocks into small bits.



What are mechanical weathering and chemical weathering?

Mechanical weathering is physical disintegration of a rock into smaller fragments, each with the same properties as the original. Chemical weathering is the process by which the internal structure of a mineral is altered by the addition or removal of elements.



What natural processes contribute to soil formation?

Parent materials.



What methods help to control soil loss?

Vegetation & Geotextiles, to name a couple.



How do erosional agents, such as water, wind, ice, and gravity, shape the landscape?

The erosional agents break up rocks, sediments, and soil from the land's surface.



What are examples of different landforms created by erosion?

Arches (pictured below), platforms, and sea stacks.



What are examples of different landforms created by deposition?

Beaches, spits, and bars.



Why is groundwater an important natural resource?

Groundwater is about 33% of of the water that water works supply to homes and businesses.



Where is groundwater stored, and how can it be obtained?

It is stored in moderately to highly permeable rocks called aquifiers. It can be obtained by digging or drilling a well.



Do we have enough groundwater to meet the demand of human beings?

No.



What are geologic epochs, periods, and eras?

An epoch is no longer than an age, but shorter than a period. A period is a point in time. An era divides eons into smaller periods of time.



What are the characteristics of the main geologic eras?

Hadean, Archean, Proterozoic, and Phanerozoic.



Which geologic laws are used to determine relative age?

Principles of Superposition & Original Horizontality, to name a couple.



What methods are used to determine absolute age?

Radiometric methods.





Glossary


Weathering

Wearing away, or changing, the appearance texture of something.



Soil

The upper layer of the Earth, where plants (including grass) grow.



Erosion

The process of eroding.



Sediment Transport

The movement of solid particles (sediment).



Deposition

The geological process in which sediments, soil, and rocks are added to a landform or land mass.



Drainage Basin

Any area of land where precipitation collects and drains off into a common outlet.



Longshore Current

An ocean current that moves parallel to shore.



Infiltration

The process by which water on the ground surface enters the soil.



Water Table

The upper level of an underground surface in which soil or rocks are permanently saturated with water.



Aquifer

A body of permeable rock that can contain and transmit groundwater.



Porosity

A measure of the empty spaces in a rock.



Absolute Dating

The process of determining an age on a specified chronology in archaeology and geology.



Relative Dating

The science of determining the relative order of past events without determining absolute age.



Uniformitarianism

The theory that changes in the earth's crust during geological history have resulted from the action of continuous and uniform processes.



Principle of Superposition

A principle that states that for all linear systems, the net response caused by two or more stimuli is the sum of the responses that would have been caused by each stimulus individually.



Unconformity

A surface of contact between two groups of unconformable strata.



Fossil

The remains or impression of a prehistoric organism preserved in petrified form or as a mold or cast in rock.
